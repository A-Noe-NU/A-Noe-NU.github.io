COVID-19 in Africa

Behind the numbers

.

.

..

.

.

.

.

Prior to the virus' spread to Africa, many experts were extremely pessimistic about how the virus would affect African nations, citing under-developed health infrastructure, widespread equipment shortages, and poverty as factors that would lead to devastating outbreaks.

Some predictions were downright apocalyptic, forecasting "nightmare scenarios" and "widespread devastation." As the pandemic progressed, initial pessimism gave way to confusion: African nations were reporting cases and deaths, but not in the same pattern as Western nations. The trendlines of some African countries showed very small peaks, while others even remained stubbornly flat. Some media outlets began to even report that Africa (all 54 countries!) was being spared the worst of the virus.

So what has actually happened? After almost two years, what can we say about how COVID-19 has spread in African nations? To try and answer that, let's first look at the official numbers.

What do the case numbers show?

The pandemic officially began in Africa in mid-February 2020 when Egypt recorded its first case, and within three months the virus had been confirmed as having spread to every African country. Here are the cumulative reported cases per capita over time in Africa. .

Throughout the pandemic, these numbers have been reported by individual, national governments in various ways (with few exceptions), and the data shown here comes from researchers at Johns Hopkins University, who tally the numbers and maintain a central database.

And here's how those numbers compare to the rest of the world. As you can see, African countries have recorded far fewer cases per capita than most of the rest of the world.

This trend was first becoming evident by July 2020, when Africa had reported only 5% of the world's cases despite accounting for about 17% of the world's population. Some initially attributed this to the virus possibly arriving in Africa later than other regions of the world, but the trend only grew more pronounced as time passed. As it did, many major Western media outlets began to speculate about whether Africa was being spared the worst of COVID-19, and why that might be so.

So has the pandemic really been different in Africa? And if so, why?

Let's look at the data another way.

Here are the reported cases each day in Africa. We can now much more easily see the three main waves that African nations have experienced so far, with each wave growing successively more intense than the last. This looks like much more like the peaks-and-valleys trend in a COVID-19 outbreak that we are all too uncomfortably familiar with now.

But here's the same data, with just three countries highlighted in green: South Africa, Morocco, and Tunisia. Combined, those three countries represent just 8% of the people who live in Africa, but they account for well over half the recorded cases. In fact, the case totals emerging from those three countries were the main drivers behind each of the three waves.

So now we can see that there was an ebb-and-flow to the outbreak in Africa like there was in other regions of the world, but that it was driven by three countries. So are other countries in Africa just undercounting cases? Or were they spared the worst of the virus?

What do the testing numbers show?

...

To understand the official case numbers reported by a country, we must also look at the tool that country uses to obtain that data: testing. So let's start by looking at the tests per capita rate that individual African nations have carried out. These numbers are reported by each individual government to the Africa Centres for Disease Control, which compiles them on their COVID-19 website.

Three countries—Cabo Verde, Gabon, and Botswana—have carried out enough tests to cover more than 50% of their population. Botswana leads all African nations with a 77% tests-per-capita ratio, reflecting an ambitious testing regimen.

But these three countries combine to represent less than 1% of Africa's population.

An additional 15 countries have carried out enough tests to cover between 10% and 50% of their population. But the population of these countries, combined, only accounts for about 10% of the actual people who live in Africa.

So what about the other 90% of people?

The majority of African nations have only been able to carry out enough tests to cover between 1% and 10% of their population. This group accounts for 70% of the people that live in Africa, or almost a billion people.

Testing for COVID-19 hasn't been readily available for many of the people in these countries for much of the pandemic. Testing kits are expensive, and they were even more expensive early on in the virus' spread when every government in the world was scrambling to acquire them. Moreover, funding for COVID-19 supplies must compete with other public health challenges. In addition, many testing centers in these countries are not at all easy to access for people that do not live close nearby. Taking a day or two off from work to travel a great distance to take a test is simply not practical for many who do not live in the capital or largest cities, especially if their symptoms are mild or nonexistent in the first place.

The final group of countries—representing the remaining 20% of Africans—have only carried out enough tests to cover less than 1% of their population. This group includes countries like Tanzania, whose president denied the reality of the outbreak up to the point when he died of the virus in March. It also includes the 92 million people who live in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, one of the most populous countries in Africa.

The massive shortage in tests and test results in these countries has not stopped Western media and politicians from repeatedly speculating about how and why Africa was potentially spared by the worst of the pandemic.





What other data do we have?

...

Luckily, scientists and doctors in Africa are much smarter than you and me; they realized the undermining of the official case numbers by low testing rates far earlier than we did; and they've been busy.

...

Researchers in a number of African countries—including Kenya, Malawi, Nigeria, South Africa, Sudan, and Zambia, to name just a few—have conducted independent studies using techniques like seroprevalence (the incidence of antibodies in a person's blood) to get at this question of the true number of cases. The results from these studies are, to put it mildly, fairly astonishing.

...

...

Researchers in South Africa (with an above average tests-per-capita rate) estimated that the true number of cases was about 10 times greater than what the country's testing regimen was catching.

...

...

Researchers in Sudan found that the number of cases in the capital city of Khartoum alone was probably about 16 times greater than what the Sudanese government had reported for the entire country up to that point.

...

...

Researchers in Zambia found that the number of people with COVID-19 antibodies was probably about 100 times greater than the official number of cases up to that point.

...

...

Researchers in Malawi found that the number of people with antibodies in the urban area of Blantyre was probably about 150 times greater than the number of cases that had been reported for the entire country up to that point.

...

...

One of the most detailed, extensive studies was carried out in Kenya. Researchers controlled for factors such as age, region, and urbanization, and found that the true number of cases was probably a whopping 620 times greater than the official tally at that point.

...

...

There are more of these, but you get the point. They all suggest a far greater number of cases in many African countries than what's reflected in the official tallies. The only question is to what extent.

But if the true number of cases is so much greater, are the number of deaths much higher as well?

Like case totals, death totals are probably not reflective of the true total: by the start of 2021, all 54 African nations had registered fewer COVID-19 deaths combined than France. But unlike case totals, the available supporting evidence for the true number of deaths is much more mixed.

Most African nations do not have official death registration systems, and family members may be buried on a private plot. And in those that do, many families do not take the extra step of reporting the death of a family member to the central government, like in Nigeria where only about 10% of deaths are reported.

Excess mortality data is one of the most reliable ways to assess COVID-19's impact, but it only exists for five countries in Africa, two of which are Mauritius and Seychelles: island countries with extremely small populations.

Both Mauritius and Seychelles have actually seen fewer deaths than would be expected during the pandemic. It's worth noting that because of their small populations, both have much higher vaccination rates than most African countries, with Seychelles in particular reaching an impressive 86% of the adult population vaccinated by early summer 2021.

The other three countries we have data for are Egypt, South Africa, and Tunisia, three of the more developed countries in Africa.

All three have seen an increase in excess mortality. South Africa's excess mortality of 228,000 is almost three times the official COVID-19 death total of 89,000. However, South Africa's population is also quite a bit older on average than the population of many African nations.

However, these excess mortality rates are not inconsistent with those of more developed countries like the United States or the United Kingdom. Nor are these five countries necessarily representative of the entirety of Africa.

The aforementioned studies in Sudan and Zambia, although not excess mortality studies, also produced some findings on COVID-19 mortality. In Sudan, researchers estimated a year ago that the pandemic at that point had killed a shocking 16,000 more people in Khartoum by then than the officially-reported total of 477.

Zambian researchers' conclusions were equally dismal. In Lusaka, scientists discovered that "contrary to expectations, deaths with COVID-19 were common." They tested 364 recently-deceased, untested people and found that 19% actually had COVID-19. Of these, about 73% had died in their community, unregistered.

Scattered anecdotal reports of unexplained deaths that are not counted in official statistics have also emerged, and the story they tell is no more encouraging. Local journalists in Kano, Nigeria, reported gravediggers working overtime to keep up with an unexplained surge in deaths in May 2020. Officials in Mogadishu, Somalia, mentioned in passing that burials have tripled as local medics told journalists about a rapid rise in deaths that were not being counted by the government. And coffin-makers and funeral directors in Tanzania reported being busier than ever before this past March, as Catholic priests reported holding far more funeral masses each day than normal. But these reports—like many of our data points—are drops in the bucket compared to the rest of Africa.

In contrast to these reports, epidemiologists have expressed tentative surprise that, by and large, hospitals in most African nations have not been overrun or stretched thin by COVID-19 patients. Proposed explanations for this include the much more youthful population, lower rates of comorbidities (like obesity and diabetes) than Western nations, and potential preexisting immunities to viruses similar to COVID-19 in Africa, but data is short and more research is still needed.

Until then, it's difficult to say how exactly to square the dismal reports of uncounted deaths with the more encouraging possibility of a lower morbidity rate in many African nations.




So what does all this mean?

It means we don't know. We don't know the true number of cases because most African nations have not been able to test enough for the virus, although most studies seem to indicate that the true number of cases in many countries is much, much higher. We don't know the true number of deaths because most African countries don't keep an official death registry, and it is not clear to what extent factors that work in many African nations' favor (like their much younger population and lower rate of comorbidities) are nullified by other factors, such as their less resourced healthcare systems. And we don't know how much we can generalize the conclusions from targeted studies in small locales to the rest of Africa's 1.2 billion people.

But even as much remains unclear, there are some facts that have been brought sharply into focus. First, endless stories built on a foundation of the official case and death totals could have used a healthy dose of skepticism injected by simply looking at the testing rates. Just as the initial apocalyptic forecasts were somewhat sensationalized, the subsequent narratives about Africa being spared by the virus ignored very real accounts of suffering and strain provided by health workers who were actually battling the virus in African locales. No, the pandemic did not bring African nations to their knees. It also did not bypass them, either. Now, those same narratives are probably contributing to a biased belief that older populations in more developed countries need vaccines more than those in African countries do. That many of us in the West are now procuring our third dose in less than a year before the vast majority of people in Africa have even had their first dose is a real tragedy—one that will continue to harm not only them, but us too, in the form of new variants like Delta and Omicron.

Second, but related to the first point: we should not only treat with great skepticism any articles that take the official case and death numbers as gospel, but acknowledge where actual glimmers of truth have come from. Real insight into the pandemic's effect on African nations, so far, has come almost exclusively from African scientists and African doctors doing hard science on the ground. Inasmuch as we in more developed countries want to actually help African nations in their struggle against COVID-19, we should be seeking to fund and support the dedicated professionals who are actually conducting research on the virus and caring for the afflicted in Africa.

Professionals like Christian Happi, a Harvard-educated Cameroonian research scientist who developed a fast-track test for COVID-19 that costs under $3. Happi directs the Africa Center of Excellence for Genomics of Infectious Diseases at Nigeria's Redeemer's University, and his team is one of the main reasons a COVID test in Nigeria can be processed in Nigeria, without having to be sent off to labs in Europe or North America.

Professionals like Mia Malan, an award-winning South African journalist who founded the Bhekisisa Centre for Health Journalism and has done much to not only educate the public about the virus, but also instruct other reporters on how to write about COVID-19 in Africa. Malan makes conversations with scientists a regular, integral part of her reporting, and she refuses to use numbers or statistics quoted by any government official unless she can source them back to an original, peer-reviewed study.

Professionals like Moses Alobo, who leads the African Academy of Sciences and has been instrumental in launching and coordinating multidisciplinary, Africa-based research efforts on how African nations can combat the virus beyond the initial steps like social distancing, testing, and wearing masks. One of Alobo's focus areas is formulating tailored plans to help manage severe cases in locales that lack intensive care facilities.

Listening to the voices of people like Happi, Malan, and Alobo—and supporting work like theirs—is one way to not only actually help fight the virus in Africa, but also ensure we have reliable, empirical data and narratives on the pandemic. In the meantime, we're left with the conclusion reached by the scientists in Zambia who found evidence of far more cases and deaths in Lukasa: "If these data are generalizable, the impact of COVID-19 in Africa has been vastly underestimated." The key word: if.
